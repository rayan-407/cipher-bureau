How the guess works

English text leans heavily on a handful of letters — E, T, A, O, and N show up far more than the rest. The tool tries every one of the 25 possible shifts, scores each result against that expected letter frequency, and ranks them. The shift with text that "looks most like English" wins — no key required. This is the classic weakness that makes a plain Caesar cipher unsafe for anything real.

What this project actually demonstrates

1. Encryption logic — each letter is mapped to another by a fixed offset around the alphabet (A→D under a shift of 3), wrapping from Z back to A. Case is preserved, and anything that isn't a letter (spaces, punctuation, digits) passes through untouched — a detail many basic implementations get wrong.

2. Decryption logic — simply shifting in the opposite direction by the same key, proving encryption and decryption are mirror operations.

3. Data protection basics — the crack tool is the real lesson: with only 25 possible keys and predictable letter frequency, Caesar ciphers can be broken in seconds without ever knowing the key. It's a live demo of why modern systems use much larger key spaces and randomized keys.

4. Logic building — the wheel visual is generated purely from the shift value, so rotating it correctly required treating the alphabet as a circular, indexable structure rather than a flat string.
